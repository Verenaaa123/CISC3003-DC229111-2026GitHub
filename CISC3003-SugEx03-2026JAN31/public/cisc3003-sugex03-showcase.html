My Statement of Purpose

My interest in computer science, particularly artificial intelligence, has grown steadily through coursework, projects, and research. Motivated by both academic curiosity and practical experience, I hope to pursue a graduate degree in computer science with a focus on AI, especially natural language processing (NLP) and machine learning. During my undergraduate studies, I built a solid foundation in programming, algorithms, and data structures before concentrating on AI. Advanced courses in natural language processing, pattern recognition, and machine learning sparked my interest in how machines interpret human language—from neural network design to models like BERT that capture semantic meaning. This interest was reinforced by projects such as using CNNs for sentiment classification and my work on data annotation in an NLP research group, where I saw how theory is applied in practice. Through these experiences, I became fascinated not only by how models learn patterns in text but also by how model design shapes linguistic understanding. They showed me that AI, and natural language processing in particular, can make human-computer communication more natural and impactful. The rapid development of transformer-based large language models and their growing ability to perform reasoning and contextual learning have convinced me that natural language processing will be central to next-generation human-computer interaction. I am eager to continue my studies and strengthen my theoretical foundations in areas such as multilingual processing and semantic understanding, while also exploring advanced techniques like few-shot learning and lightweight model design. Ultimately, I hope to apply AI to practical challenges such as cross-border communication and intelligent customer service, helping machines understand language and provide effective support. In addition to my initial exposure to natural language processing and machine learning, I strengthened my academic foundation by taking a series of core and advanced computer science courses. Courses in machine learning, pattern recognition, computer vision, and web search gave me both deeper theoretical understanding and hands-on experience. For instance, in a social-media text analysis project inspired by real-world platforms, I used TF-IDF and Random Forest models to identify relationships between trending topics and public sentiment. This allowed me to explore data preprocessing, feature engineering, and model fine-tuning, which are essential skills for NLP tasks such as topic modeling and text classification. I also studied data structures and algorithms through Gregory Kesden’s CMU online courses, which emphasized formal logic, proofs of correctness, and performance analysis. These lessons taught me the importance of rigorous reasoning and efficient representation—concepts that also support model optimization and explainability in modern AI. I am proud of this education, which has given me the scientific foundation and critical thinking skills I hope to apply in graduate study in cutting-edge AI fields. My research has motivated me to delve deeper into NLP, shifting my curiosity from classroom theory to real-world problems. I joined my advisor’s research lab, NLP2CT, to work on mathematical reasoning projects aimed at improving the compositional reasoning ability of large language models (LLMs). My responsibilities included preparing task reports and conducting model training and fine-tuning experiments. Specifically, I investigated reinforcement learning (RL) and direct preference optimization (DPO) methods to achieve higher accuracy using simpler models and fewer computational resources. The project was challenging. The dataset I initially had was limited, so I needed to generate much of the data, and extracting and quantifying mathematical formulas with nested structures was complex. To address these difficulties, I helped design a new dataset framework in Python and proposed metrics to evaluate expression complexity. Tackling these problems and constructing a new dataset that explicitly considered them required significant problem-solving skills as well as creative data design. This work not only strengthened my ability to write and implement algorithms in Python and lightweight frameworks, but also deepened my understanding of large-scale language models, including multimodal models, their design motivations, and methods for fine-tuning them. Through this project, I realized that improving model reasoning often depends less on architectural changes than on how data complexity and intermediate representations are structured—an insight that continues to guide my research thinking. During my summer internship in the Information Systems Department at Electricidade de Macau (EMC), I assisted with daily project operations and the annual CMDB inventory project, which involved recording the serial numbers, quantities, and locations of devices (such as desktops and laptops) across departments. Initially, the project faced numerous challenges: manual verification was slow, error-prone, and difficult to scale across multiple locations. Attempts to digitize the process using online surveys ultimately failed due to inconsistent feedback, complex approval processes, and difficulties in mapping departments and equipment categories. To address these issues, I helped optimize the Excel-based backend scripts for data management. I streamlined the form logic by restructuring conditional questions and removing redundant fields, added drop-down menus, automated asset ID formatting, and implemented real-time error checks. I also automated departmental device mapping based on user login information and embedded dynamic help tips based on device type. The improved scripts significantly enhanced system usability, streamlined the inventory process, and improved accuracy. This experience taught me practical methods for using computational tools to improve operational efficiency and provided me with end-to-end exposure, from problem identification to technical implementation. More importantly, it showed me how computational thinking—decomposing a complex workflow into logic-driven modules—mirrors the way AI systems learn to automate decision processes.

Key points

  • I aspire to deepen my expertise in artificial intelligence and natural language processing while refining my technical and analytical skills.
  • I hope to contribute to accessible NLP technologies that bridge language barriers and make AI communication systems more inclusive and human-centered.
